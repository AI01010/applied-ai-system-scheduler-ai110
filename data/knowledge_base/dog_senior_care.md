# Senior Dog Care (7+ years, breed-dependent)

Large breeds enter their senior years around 6–7, while small breeds may not slow noticeably until 10–12. Watch for the soft signs: a longer warm-up period in the morning, hesitation on stairs, slower response to recall, cloudier eyes, or graying around the muzzle.

Feeding should shift toward smaller, more frequent meals if your senior shows reduced appetite or signs of reflux. Many vets recommend splitting the adult two-meal schedule into three:

- 07:30 — Light meal
- 13:00 — Small midday meal
- 18:30 — Evening meal (avoid feeding within 2 hours of bedtime)

Lower-calorie senior formulas help offset slowing metabolism, but joint supplements (typically glucosamine/chondroitin/omega-3) should only be added on veterinary advice.

Exercise should be more frequent and lower-intensity. Replace one 45-minute brisk walk with three 15-minute gentle walks at 08:00, 13:00, and 17:00. Soft surfaces (grass, dirt trails) are easier on arthritic joints than concrete.

Sleep increases — expect 16–18 hours per day, often with deeper afternoon naps. Provide an orthopedic bed in a quiet, draft-free location, and resist waking a sleeping senior unless necessary.

Cognitive enrichment becomes more important as the body slows. A 5-minute scent game with hidden kibble at 14:00 keeps the mind engaged without taxing the body. Regular routines reduce confusion in dogs developing canine cognitive dysfunction.

Schedule veterinary check-ups every six months rather than annually. Bloodwork can catch kidney, liver, and thyroid changes early.

This guidance is advisory only. Any new limp, change in appetite, increased thirst, or behavioral shift warrants a vet visit, not a schedule tweak.
