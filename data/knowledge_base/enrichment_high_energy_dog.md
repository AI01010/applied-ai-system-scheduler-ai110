# Enrichment for High-Energy Dogs

High-energy dogs are not "bad dogs" — they are under-employed dogs. A 60-minute walk burns about as many calories as 15 minutes of focused mental work. Used together, they make a settled dog. Used apart, you get a dog who chews the couch.

## Daily mental-enrichment menu (rotate to avoid boredom)

- **Snuffle mat for breakfast.** Hide kibble in fabric strips. Engages the strongest sense (smell) and slows eating.
- **Frozen Kong.** Stuff with wet food, peanut butter (xylitol-free!), and freeze. 15–30 min of intense focus.
- **Find-it games.** Hide treats around the house in escalating difficulty.
- **Training session.** 5–10 min of impulse-control work (stay, leave it, place) is genuinely tiring for the dog.
- **Puzzle feeders.** Rotate 2–3 different ones; same puzzle gets boring fast.
- **Lick mat.** Spread soft treats on a textured silicone mat — calming, takes 10–15 min.
- **Cardboard box destruction.** Hide treats in shredded paper inside a sealed box. Acceptable destruction, satisfying outlet.

## Second exercise window

For very high-energy dogs (working breeds, intact young adults), one walk per day is rarely enough. Schedule a second short exercise window — even 20 minutes of fetch in the yard or a brisk neighborhood walk before dinner.

## Adolescent dogs (8–24 months)

This age group is the hardest. They have adult bodies, puppy brains, and undeveloped impulse control. Expect 2 walks/day plus 30+ minutes of training and enrichment. The behavior pattern will normalize after age 2 in most breeds, age 3 in working lines.

## What does not count as enrichment

- Television
- A toy left in the room while you ignore the dog
- Off-leash backyard time without a thrown ball or game
- Long car rides
