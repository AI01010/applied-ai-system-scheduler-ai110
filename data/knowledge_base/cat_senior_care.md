# Senior Cat Care (10+ years)

Senior cats need gentler routines but still benefit from engagement. Joint stiffness, reduced kidney function, and dental issues are the most common age-related changes.

Daily structure:

- 07:00 — Senior or kidney-formula wet food (small portion, easier to digest than dry)
- 07:15 — Litter box scoop; watch for changes in volume or color
- 11:00 — Brief gentle play (5–10 min); a wand kept low to the ground avoids jumping strain
- 14:00 — Sun-spot nap window — make sure a warm soft bed is accessible without jumping
- 18:00 — Second wet food meal
- 19:00 — Grooming session: senior cats often groom less, and gentle brushing prevents mats and feels good

Make food and water bowls accessible without stairs. A second water station near common rest spots increases intake — important for kidney health. Consider raised bowls if arthritis is suspected.

Litter boxes should have low entry sides for easy access, and there should be at least one per floor of the home.

Twice-yearly vet visits become important after age 10. Bloodwork catches early kidney disease, hyperthyroidism, and diabetes before symptoms are visible. Weight loss in a senior cat is rarely benign — check with your vet.

Note: this is general guidance, not medical advice. Consult your veterinarian for any health concerns specific to your cat.
