# Hot-Weather Pet Care

Heat stroke is a leading cause of summer pet emergencies. Dogs cannot sweat efficiently — they lose heat primarily through panting. A dog in a 90°F (32°C) car can suffer brain damage in 15 minutes; a sustained walk on hot pavement can cause paw burns in 60 seconds.

## Walking in hot weather

- Test pavement: place the back of your hand on the asphalt for 7 seconds. If it's too hot for you, it's too hot for paws.
- Walk before 09:00 or after 19:00 in summer
- Carry water for any walk over 15 minutes; small dogs need water every 10 minutes
- Avoid mid-day walks entirely if temperature + humidity exceeds 100 (e.g., 85°F + 75% humidity)
- Brachycephalic breeds (Bulldogs, Pugs, Frenchies, Boxers) cannot pant efficiently and are at very high risk — keep walks under 10 minutes in heat

## Signs of heat stroke (emergency)

- Heavy panting that does not slow even at rest
- Bright red gums or tongue
- Drooling thick saliva
- Disorientation, stumbling, collapse
- Vomiting

If you suspect heat stroke: move to shade or A/C, apply cool (not ice cold) water to belly and paw pads, offer small sips of water, and drive to an emergency vet immediately. Do not delay to "see if it gets better".

## Schedule adjustments

The PawPal+ scheduler does not check the weather, but the owner should manually shift summer schedules:
- Move morning walks 60 minutes earlier
- Move evening walks 60 minutes later
- Replace mid-day walk with mental enrichment (puzzle feeder, training session)
- For high-energy dogs, consider a swim session as cardio replacement

Cars: never leave a pet in a parked car in summer, even with windows cracked, even for "5 minutes". This is the single most preventable summer pet emergency.

This is general safety information, not medical advice.
