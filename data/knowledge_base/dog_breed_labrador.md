# Labrador Retriever Care

Labradors are the most popular companion breed in many countries for good reason: they are biddable, social, and tolerant. They are also genetically prone to obsessive eating behavior — a recognized POMC gene variant in many Labs blunts the satiety response. Translation: a Lab will tell you it is starving five minutes after dinner. Believe the bowl, not the dog.

Feeding:

- 07:00 — Measured breakfast (use a kitchen scale, not a scoop)
- 18:00 — Measured dinner
- Avoid free-feeding. Avoid table scraps. A Lab at ideal body condition has a visible waist and palpable but not visible ribs.

Exercise needs are substantial through age 7. Adult working-line Labs need 60–90 minutes of vigorous activity daily; show-line Labs are slightly calmer but still high-energy. Swimming is ideal — most Labs love water, and it spares hips and elbows.

A standard daily structure:

- 06:30 — 30–45 minute walk or swim
- 12:30 — Midday potty break with 10 minutes of fetch
- 17:30 — 30–45 minute second walk or training session
- 19:30 — Frozen Kong or chew toy

Health priorities: hip and elbow dysplasia screening, weight management, and ear care. Labs with floppy ears and water exposure are prone to ear infections — dry ears thoroughly after swimming and check weekly for redness or odor.

Adolescence (8–18 months) is the hardest phase; Labs hit a "teenager" period where recall and impulse control regress. Stay consistent; this passes.

Grooming is light but real: brush twice weekly, daily during the major spring shed. Labs have a water-resistant double coat — do not shave.

This is general guidance. Consult your veterinarian for portion sizing and joint screening.
