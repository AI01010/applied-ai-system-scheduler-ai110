# Shift Worker with a Cat

Cats adapt to inverted schedules better than dogs, but they still need predictable feeding times. A shift worker (e.g., 22:00–06:00 night shift) can build a routine that works for both.

Night-shift owner sample schedule:

- 06:30 — Owner returns home; cat first meal of the day
- 07:00 — 10 min interactive play to bond after long absence
- 07:30 — Owner sleeps; cat curls up nearby (or in their own bed)
- 14:00 — Owner wakes; brief play + treat
- 15:00 — Litter box scoop, water refresh, second feeding
- 16:00–21:00 — Owner's "evening" / personal time; cat gets passive enrichment (window perch, food puzzle)
- 21:00 — Final play session before owner leaves
- 21:30 — Owner leaves for work
- (cat sleeps overnight; this is fine — cats sleep 14–16 hours/day naturally)

Key principles:
- Two solid feedings per 24-hour cycle, evenly spaced
- The "evening" play session before owner leaves is critical — it gives the cat a hunt-eat-groom-sleep cycle that mimics nature, helping them sleep through owner-absence hours
- An automatic feeder for a small mid-cycle snack can help cats with diabetes or those who beg constantly

Shift workers often report cats who "won't stop pestering" during sleep — usually means the play session is too short or skipped. Cats are obligate carnivores; they NEED to express predatory behavior daily, and a wand toy session is the easiest way to satisfy that drive.

Avoid shifting feed times by more than 1 hour from the established routine. Cats are creatures of habit and will pace, vocalize, or stop eating if their schedule changes too abruptly.
