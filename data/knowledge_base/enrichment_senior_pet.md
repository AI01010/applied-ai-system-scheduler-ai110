# Gentle Enrichment for Senior Pets

Senior pets — dogs over 8, cats over 11, depending on breed and size — slow down physically but still benefit enormously from mental stimulation. "Less exercise" does not mean "no engagement". Bored seniors deteriorate cognitively faster.

## Daily gentle enrichment

- **Two short walks** (dogs): 10–15 min each, slow pace, sniffing time. Sniffing is more mentally engaging than distance covered.
- **Lick mats and puzzle feeders** at a low difficulty setting. Avoid puzzles requiring jumping or pawing at speed.
- **Massage and grooming** as quality time. Senior pets often groom themselves less; gentle brushing is bonding and prevents mats.
- **Scent enrichment.** New smells — a piece of clothing from a friend, a leaf from a hike — engage the brain without physical exertion.
- **Low-stimulation training.** Refresh "sit", "shake", or recall in short 3-minute sessions. Senior dogs love being praised for things they already know.
- **Window time** for cats. A senior cat watching birds at a window is engaged in passive but real cognitive activity.

## Pain-aware adjustments

- Use raised food and water bowls if neck arthritis is suspected
- Provide soft beds in multiple rooms — joints stiffen on hard floors
- Avoid stairs when possible; carry small dogs/cats up if they hesitate
- Skip slippery floors; runners or rugs help with traction

## Cognitive decline signs

Senior pets can develop a form of dementia (Cognitive Dysfunction Syndrome). Early signs include:
- Confusion in familiar spaces
- Sleep-wake cycle disturbance (awake at night, sleeping all day)
- Reduced response to their name
- House-soiling that wasn't a previous issue

These warrant a vet visit. There are interventions (diet changes, supplements your vet may discuss) but earlier is better.

This document is general guidance. Anything health-related — including pain management — should be discussed with your veterinarian.
